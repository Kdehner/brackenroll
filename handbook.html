Player Handbook

Everything You Need to Play

Daggerheart runs on a conversation loop: describe what you do, roll when the outcome is uncertain and interesting, find out what happens. Every roll produces two things at once — an outcome for you, and an outcome for the world.

When to Roll

Roll When…

The outcome is uncertain and interesting to the story. Your GM calls for a roll when success and failure would both move the fiction forward in meaningfully different ways.

Don't roll for things that are trivially easy or truly impossible — the GM already knows the result.

No Initiative — No Rounds

There is no initiative order or turn structure. The spotlight passes organically. Players act until someone fails a roll or rolls with Fear — then the GM takes a turn to spotlight adversaries. The GM can also spend Fear to interrupt players and spotlight enemies.

A spotlighted adversary moves, attacks, or uses an ability. Then play returns to the players.

The Six Traits

Every action roll uses one of your six traits. Your GM tells you which one fits what you're doing. Add your trait modifier to both dice when you roll.

Agility

Speed, reflexes, and movement. Rolling to sprint, dodge, leap, or sneak.

Strength

Raw physical force. Rolling to lift, grapple, break, or overpower something.

Finesse

Precision and dexterity. Rolling for ranged attacks, lockpicking, sleight of hand.

Instinct

Awareness and intuition. Rolling to perceive danger, track, or read a situation.

Presence

Charisma and force of will. Rolling to persuade, intimidate, deceive, or perform.

Knowledge

Learning and recall. Rolling to identify magic, remember lore, or apply expertise.

The Duality Dice

Making an Action Roll

Roll two d12s — a Hope Die and a Fear Die. Add your trait modifier and any other bonuses. Compare to the Difficulty set by your GM.

All bonuses must be declared before you roll — you can't add them after seeing the result.

The two dice also determine whether you or the GM gains a resource, regardless of success or failure.

Attack & Spellcast Rolls

Attack rolls use your weapon's listed trait and compare against the target's Evasion as the Difficulty.

Spellcast rolls use your subclass's Spellcast Trait (shown on your subclass card). Only spells that specifically call for a Spellcast Roll use this — it cannot be substituted for other rolls.

Unarmed attacks use Strength or Finesse and deal d4 physical damage using your Proficiency.

Reaction Rolls

Some adversary attacks and spell effects call for a reaction roll — you roll to resist or avoid the effect. Use the same duality dice mechanic as an action roll, but reaction rolls don't generate Hope or Fear.

On a critical success on a reaction roll, you ignore even the partial effects that normally apply on a success.

You can spend Hope to add an applicable Experience modifier to a reaction roll.

Advantage & Disadvantage

Advantage: add a d6 to your total.
Disadvantage: subtract a d6 from your total.

Advantage and disadvantage cancel each other out one-for-one. If you have two sources of advantage and one of disadvantage, the result is one advantage die.

You never roll both at the same time — they always cancel before resolving.

Hope

Hope is your resource — earned when your Hope Die rolls higher than your Fear Die. You can hold up to 6 Hope at a time, and it carries between sessions.

Spend Hope to:

  • Give an ally a d6 advantage die (1 Hope) — multiple allies can help simultaneously
  • Add an Experience modifier to a roll (1 Hope)
  • Initiate a Tag Team Roll with an ally (3 Hope)
  • Activate your class's Hope Feature (usually 3 Hope)

Fear

When your Fear Die rolls higher, the GM gains a Fear token — even if you succeeded. Fear isn't failure; it's the world pushing back.

The GM spends Fear to spotlight adversaries, trigger complications, introduce new threats, or activate danger moves.

On a short rest the GM gains 1d4 Fear. On a long rest the GM gains 1d4 + number of PCs Fear.

Stress

Stress is mental and physical strain. All characters start with 6 Stress slots.

Mark a Stress to: add a d6 advantage die to any action roll, or activate certain features.

When you mark your last Stress slot, you become Vulnerable until you clear at least 1 Stress. If you're forced to mark Stress when all slots are full, mark 1 HP instead.

Stress clears on a critical success or during downtime.

Critical Success Damage

When both dice show the same number, it's a critical success — automatic success, gain 1 Hope, clear 1 Stress.

On a critical attack, you deal extra damage: set your damage dice to their maximum possible value, then roll them again and add the roll to the maximum. For example, a d8 weapon deals 8 + 1d8 damage on a critical.

What Your Roll Means

Critical
Both dice show the same number — automatic success, gain 1 Hope, clear 1 Stress. On an attack, deal maximum dice value + roll again.
Success with Hope
Total meets Difficulty and Hope Die is higher — you succeed and gain 1 Hope. Play stays with the players.
Success with Fear
Total meets Difficulty but Fear Die is higher — you succeed, but the GM gains 1 Fear and play passes to them. Something complicates the moment.
Failure with Hope
Total falls short but Hope Die is higher — you fail, but gain 1 Hope. Play stays with the players. The door didn't open, but you found something useful.
Failure with Fear
Total falls short and Fear Die is higher — you fail and the GM gains 1 Fear. Play passes to the GM. Never the end of the story, but never easy either.

Hit Points & Damage

Taking Damage

When an adversary succeeds on an attack against your Evasion, they roll their damage and compare it to your damage thresholds.

  • Below Major threshold → Minor: mark 1 HP
  • Meets or exceeds Major → Major: mark 2 HP
  • Meets or exceeds Severe → Severe: mark 3 HP

Your thresholds = armor base + your current level.

Armor Slots

When you take damage, you can mark an Armor Slot to reduce the damage severity by one tier:

  • Severe → Major (2 HP instead of 3)
  • Major → Minor (1 HP instead of 2)
  • Minor → No damage

You can only mark one Armor Slot per attack. Armor Slots are repaired during downtime. Direct damage (stated explicitly on some abilities) cannot be reduced by Armor Slots.

Conditions

Conditions are temporary states applied by adversary moves, class features, or domain cards. Unless stated otherwise, a condition lasts until the end of the current scene or until the affected creature takes an action to clear it.

Vulnerable

All action and reaction rolls made against you have advantage. You become Vulnerable when you mark your last Stress slot, and clear it when you clear at least 1 Stress.

Hidden

You can't be directly targeted by attacks. Attackers must guess your location or use special abilities to find you. Your first attack from Hidden automatically has advantage.

Restrained

You can't move. Rolls to attack you have advantage, and your own attack rolls have disadvantage. Clearing Restrained usually requires an action roll or an ally's help.

Stunned

You can't take actions or reactions. You can't move. Rolls against you have advantage. Clears when you take an action to pull yourself together or at the end of the scene.

On Fire

When a creature acts while On Fire, they must take extra fire damage (amount specified by the effect) if they are still On Fire at the end of their action.

Enraptured

Your attention is fixed on the source. Your field of view narrows and you can only hear the voice of the caster. Certain actions force you to mark Stress or end the condition.

Downtime & Rests

When the party rests, each character makes 2 downtime moves. A party can take up to 3 short rests before their next rest must be a long rest. If a short rest is interrupted, no benefits are gained. If a long rest is interrupted, treat it as a short rest instead.

Short Rest

~1 hour — 2 downtime moves each. GM gains 1d4 Fear.
Tend to Wounds — Clear a number of HP equal to 1d4 + your tier. You can do this to an ally's HP instead.
Clear Stress — Describe how you blow off steam, then clear a number of Stress equal to 1d4 + your tier.
Repair Armor — Clear a number of Armor Slots equal to 1d4 + your tier. You can do this to an ally's armor instead.
Prepare — Gain 1 Hope. If you Prepare with one or more allies, you each gain 2 Hope instead.

Long Rest

Full camp — 2 downtime moves each. GM gains 1d4 + number of PCs Fear.
Tend to All Wounds — Clear all marked HP. You can do this to an ally instead.
Clear All Stress — Clear all marked Stress.
Repair All Armor — Clear all Armor Slots. You can do this to an ally's armor instead.
Prepare — Gain 1 Hope, or 2 Hope if preparing with allies.
Work on a Project — Advance a long-term crafting, research, or personal goal with GM guidance.

Swapping Domain Cards at Rest

At the start of a rest, before making any downtime moves, you can freely swap domain cards between your loadout and vault. Features that last "until your next rest" end when you finish resting. Features marked "once per rest" refresh. Features marked "once per session" only refresh at the start of your next session.

Death Move

When you mark your last Hit Point, you make a death move. Choose one of the three options below.

Blaze of Glory

Embrace death and go out spectacularly. Take one final action at the GM's discretion — it automatically critically succeeds. Then your character crosses through the veil of death.

Avoid Death

Your character drops unconscious. Work with the GM to describe how the situation worsens. While unconscious you can't move or act, can't be targeted by attacks, and return to consciousness when an ally clears 1 or more of your HP or when the party finishes a long rest.

You may take a Scar — cross out one Hope slot permanently. It can't be used anymore.

Risk It All

Roll your Duality Dice. If the Hope Die is higher: stay on your feet and clear HP or Stress equal to the Hope Die value (divide as you choose). If the Fear Die is higher: cross through the veil of death. On a critical, you survive.

Scars

A Scar crosses out a Hope slot permanently. The narrative form is up to you — a physical wound, a painful memory, a deep fear. Scars can be healed as a downtime project or a quest reward at the GM's discretion. If you ever cross out your last Hope slot, it's time to end your character's journey — work with your GM to find a fitting farewell.

Domain Cards

Domain cards are your abilities — spells, techniques, and powers drawn from your class's two domains. Your subclass, ancestry, and community cards are always active and don't count toward your loadout limit.

Loadout & Vault

You can have up to 5 domain cards active in your loadout at one time. Any card in your loadout is available to use.

Your vault holds cards you own but haven't equipped. Some features permanently place a card in your vault, removing it from play.

Swapping Cards

At the start of a rest (before downtime moves), freely swap cards between loadout and vault.

Mid-session swaps: mark Stress equal to the card's Recall Cost (⚡ number on the card). Swap it in for a card in your loadout.

Usage Limits & Tokens

Cards with once per rest / once per long rest / once per session limits should be marked when used (exhausted). The sheet tracks this with the "Use" toggle.

Cards that say "place tokens" use the token counter on the sheet. Tokens persist between sessions — record them if needed.

Card Types

Spells require a Spellcast Roll using your subclass's Spellcast Trait. They deal magic damage unless stated otherwise.

Abilities are techniques or passive powers. Some trigger automatically; others need Hope, Stress, or an action.

Grimoires contain multiple named powers — any can be used while the grimoire is in your loadout.

Your class defines what you're built for. Each class has two subclasses — you choose one at character creation. Click any class to see full details including subclass features.

Domain Cards & Your Loadout

  • You can have at most 5 domain cards active at a time. Extras go into your vault.
  • At the start of a rest, swap cards freely between loadout and vault before taking downtime moves.
  • When multiclassing, domain cards from your new class are limited to half your current level (rounded up).

Weapons deal physical or magic damage using your Proficiency. Your damage roll uses the listed dice — add modifiers where shown. Armor sets your damage thresholds (Minor/Major/Severe = armor base + your level).

Reading a Weapon

Trait — which trait you roll for attacks.
Range — how far it reaches.
Damage — dice rolled on a hit, using your Proficiency dice count.
Burden — One-Handed or Two-Handed.
Feature — special rule for this weapon.

You have a primary and optional secondary weapon slot. Switching weapons mid-combat marks 1 Stress; switching at rest is free.

Reading Armor

Minor / Major Threshold — base values; add your current level to each during play.
Severe Threshold — always Major × 2.
Armor Score — number of Armor Slots you have.
Feature — special rule for this armor.

Wearing no armor: Minor threshold = your level, Severe = Major × 2.

The whole party levels up together when your GM calls a milestone. Most groups play at least three sessions between levels. Characters go from level 1 to 10 across four tiers of play.

Tiers of Play

Tier 1

Level 1

Starting characters. Establishing your identity, early abilities, and how you fit into the world.

Tier 2

Levels 2–4

Early growth. Proficiency increases at level 2, new domain cards, building toward your subclass specialization.

Tier 3

Levels 5–7

Mid-tier power. Subclass upgrades become available. Proficiency increase and multiclassing cost both advancement picks.

Tier 4

Levels 8–10

High-tier play. Mastery features, subclass mastery upgrade, peak character potential.

When You Level Up

Always — Damage Thresholds +1

Your damage thresholds increase by 1 every level (they always equal armor base + current level). Update your Major and Severe threshold values on your sheet.

Always — Take a Domain Card

Take one new domain card at your level or lower from a domain your class has access to. If you're over 5 cards, vault the excess. This is separate from the "Additional Domain Card" advancement option.

Level Achievements (Levels 2, 5, and 8 Only) — Automatic

At levels 2, 5, and 8, these bonuses apply automatically — they don't cost advancement picks:

  • A new Experience at +2 modifier (you name it)
  • A permanent +1 to your Proficiency (also increases weapon damage dice count)
  • At levels 5 and 8: clear all marks on character traits you've previously increased — those traits can be raised again this tier

Choose 2 Advancements

Pick two from the list below. Some have a per-tier limit. Tier 3+ adds additional options. Options that cost both picks use up your entire 2-pick budget for that level.

  • +1 to Two Character Traits — choose two unmarked traits, mark them (can't increase again until tier resets at level 5 or 8). Max 3 times per tier.
  • +1 Hit Point Slot — permanently gain one HP slot. Max 2 times per tier.
  • +1 Stress Slot — permanently gain one Stress slot. Max 2 times per tier.
  • +1 to Two Experiences — increase two existing experience modifiers by +1 each. Max 2 times per tier.
  • Additional Domain Card — take one extra domain card at your level or lower. Max 1 per tier.
  • +1 Evasion — permanently increase Evasion. Max 1 per tier.

Tier 3+ Additional Options (Levels 5–10)

  • Upgrade Subclass Card — take the next tier of your subclass (Foundation → Specialization → Mastery). Max 1 per tier. Exclusive with Multiclass that tier.
  • +1 Proficiency — costs both advancement picks. Max 1 per tier.
  • Multiclass — choose a second class, gain access to one of its domains (limited to half your level rounded up), and take a Foundation feature from one of its subclasses. Costs both picks. Only once per character total. Exclusive with Subclass Upgrade that tier.

Experiences

Experiences represent skills, knowledge, or history your character brings that don't fit neatly into the six traits. You start with two, gain one at each level achievement, and can raise their modifier over time. A good Experience is specific and character-driven — not a mechanical repeat of a trait.

Using an Experience

  • Spend 1 Hope before you roll to add your Experience modifier to an action or reaction roll where it applies
  • Multiple Experiences can apply to the same roll if they're each genuinely relevant — spend Hope for each one
  • Wizards (School of Knowledge) can mark a Stress instead of spending Hope and double the modifier for that roll
  • Experiences can be changed when they no longer fit the character — work with your GM
